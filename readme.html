01 / FOUNDATIONS

History & Origins

Candlestick charting is the oldest surviving form of technical analysis — predating Western bar charts by over a century.

In the early 1700s, a Japanese rice merchant named Munehisa Homma (本間宗久) observed that rice prices were driven not only by supply and demand, but by the emotional states of traders — fear, greed, and indecision. He began recording price movements in a visual format that captured the open, high, low, and close of each trading session, creating what we now call the candlestick chart.

Homma is said to have completed over 100 consecutive winning trades using his method, amassing a fortune equivalent to hundreds of millions of dollars in today's terms. His insights were published in two works: Sakata Senho (Rules of Sakata) and Soba Sani No Den (The Fountain of Gold).

~1700s
Munehisa Homma develops the candlestick method for the Dojima Rice Exchange in Osaka, Japan.
1700–1867
The Tokugawa Shogunate era. Candlestick terminology draws heavily from military strategy — "three white soldiers," "gravestone," "counterattack lines."
1989
Steve Nison publishes his landmark article introducing candlestick charting to Western markets, triggering immediate widespread adoption.
1991
Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques is published — the definitive Western reference text, now required reading for the CMT program.
Today
Virtually every charting platform defaults to candlestick charts. The method is core to the CMT Body of Knowledge and is used across all asset classes globally.
CMT Principle

The CMT Association emphasizes that candlestick patterns reflect market psychology in action. Each pattern is a visual record of the battle between buyers and sellers within a defined time period. Patterns must always be interpreted in the context of the prevailing trend and surrounding price structure.

02 / FOUNDATIONS

Candle Anatomy

Every candlestick encodes four data points — open, high, low, close (OHLC) — into a single visual element.

The Four Price Points

Open
First traded price of the period. In a bullish candle, this is the bottom of the body. In bearish, the top.
High
Highest price reached. The tip of the upper shadow. Represents maximum intraperiod bullish pressure.
Low
Lowest price reached. The tip of the lower shadow. Represents maximum intraperiod bearish pressure.
Close
Final traded price. The most important of the four — it reflects the market's final verdict for the period.
CMT Key Principle

The close is the most psychologically significant price point. Many institutional trading systems (moving averages, breakout confirmations) are based on closing prices. The Japanese called late-session manipulation of the close a "night attack" — a deliberate strategy to influence the candlestick's final appearance.

Shadow Interpretation

The length and position of shadows carry critical information about intraperiod battles between bulls and bears:

  • Long upper shadow — Buyers drove price high, but sellers rejected the advance. Bearish implication.
  • Long lower shadow — Sellers drove price down, but buyers absorbed all supply and recovered. Bullish implication.
  • No shadows (Marubozu) — One side dominated the entire session without any meaningful counterattack.
  • Equal shadows — Market indecision; neither side could maintain an advantage.

Timeframe Considerations

Every candlestick represents one complete period of price action. A daily candle captures the entire trading session; a 5-minute candle captures only those five minutes. The same pattern on a weekly chart carries far more weight than on a 1-minute chart because it represents more collective decision-making and has been tested by more market participants.

The Doji Family

A doji forms when open ≈ close. It is the purest expression of market indecision — but context determines whether it is a reversal or continuation signal.

CMT Insight

A doji has far more significance after a prolonged trend than during a sideways market. In consolidation, dojis are expected. After a sustained uptrend, a doji warns that bullish momentum has stalled and sellers are beginning to find their footing.

Standard Doji
Neutral
Open = close; roughly equal upper and lower shadows forming a cross. Pure market indecision. The classic signal of a trend losing momentum.
50%
Long-Legged Doji
Neutral

Bullish

07 / SINGLE CANDLE

Spinning Top

Similar to a doji but with a visible body. A small real body with long equal shadows on both sides signals indecision and a potential trend pause or reversal.

Bullish
Spinning Top
Bearish
Spinning Top

Key Characteristics

  • Small real body (bull or bear) centered between long shadows
  • Both shadows roughly equal length — balanced battle
  • Significant only when appearing after a trend, not in consolidation
  • Bullish spinning top after a downtrend = bulls beginning to fight back
  • Bearish spinning top after an uptrend = bears beginning to resist
  • Always requires confirmation from the next candle
Common Error

Spinning tops alone do not constitute a trade signal. They are alerts to watch for a follow-through candle. Acting on a spinning top without confirmation has very low reliability (~50%).

Hammer & Hanging Man

The same shape, two opposite signals. Context — the prior trend — determines everything. This is the classic example of how candlestick patterns cannot be evaluated in isolation.

Hammer
BULLISH REVERSAL
Appears at the bottom of a downtrend. Long lower shadow (≥2× the body) shows sellers pushed price aggressively lower, but buyers stepped in and drove it back up near the open. The bulls are beginning to win the battle.
Must appear after a downtrend

Little or no upper shadow

Hanging Man
BEARISH REVERSAL
Identical shape, but appears at the top of an uptrend. The long lower shadow shows that sellers successfully pushed prices down significantly during the session — a warning that bears are awakening. The eventual recovery doesn't guarantee continuation.
Must appear after an uptrend

CMT Key Insight — Context Is Everything

A hammer and hanging man share the identical visual structure. The only difference is where they appear in a trend. This illustrates the most fundamental principle in candlestick analysis: patterns cannot be interpreted without knowledge of the prior price action.

10 / TWO-CANDLE

Engulfing Patterns

Among the most reliable two-candle reversal patterns. The second candle completely consumes the body of the first, signaling a decisive shift in market control.

Bullish Engulfing
BULLISH REVERSAL · ~63% Reliability
A green candle completely engulfs the body of the prior red candle. Bulls have overwhelmed bears. The larger the engulfing candle relative to the first, the more powerful the signal.
Day 1: bearish candle (any size)
Day 2: opens lower than Day 1 close
Day 2: closes above Day 1 open
Volume on Day 2 ideally higher
Bearish Engulfing
BEARISH REVERSAL · ~63% Reliability
A red candle completely engulfs the body of the prior green candle. Sellers have overwhelmed buyers decisively. More powerful when it appears at key resistance after a significant advance.
Day 1: bullish candle
Day 2: opens above Day 1 close
Day 2: closes below Day 1 open
Gap up open on Day 2 strengthens
Signal Strength Factors

Engulfing patterns are stronger when: (1) the engulfing body is significantly larger than the prior candle, (2) they appear after a prolonged trend, (3) the pattern forms at a significant support or resistance level, (4) volume is higher on the engulfing day, and (5) the open of the engulfing candle gaps away from the prior close.

11 / TWO-CANDLE

Harami

Japanese for "pregnant." The opposite of the engulfing — the second candle is entirely contained within the first. A weaker reversal signal that requires confirmation.

Bullish Harami
BULLISH REVERSAL · Requires Confirmation
A large bearish candle is followed by a smaller bullish candle whose body is entirely within the first. Selling momentum has stalled. The bulls have appeared, but haven't yet proven dominance.
Harami Cross
STRONGER SIGNAL · Doji Version
A large candle followed by a doji contained within its range. Considered stronger than the standard harami because the doji confirms complete indecision — the trend may be about to reverse.
12 / TWO-CANDLE

Piercing Line & Dark Cloud Cover

Two-candle patterns where the second candle penetrates at least halfway into the body of the first. The halfway threshold is the critical validation rule.

Piercing Line
BULLISH REVERSAL · After Downtrend
Day 1: long bearish candle. Day 2: opens below Day 1's low (gap down), then rallies to close above the midpoint of Day 1's body. The deeper the penetration, the stronger the signal.
Dark Cloud Cover
BEARISH REVERSAL · After Uptrend
Day 1: long bullish candle. Day 2: opens above Day 1's high (gap up), then sells off to close below the midpoint of Day 1's body. The false gap-up traps late bulls.
Validation Rule

If the second candle closes less than 50% into the prior candle's body, neither pattern is valid. Some practitioners require a 2/3 penetration for a high-confidence signal. Always measure body-to-body, not including shadows.

13 / TWO-CANDLE

Tweezer Tops & Bottoms

Two candles that test the same extreme price level. The market attempted to move past a price twice and was rejected both times — a dual confirmation of support or resistance.

Tweezer Bottom
BULLISH REVERSAL
Two candles share the same low. The first is bearish; the second is bullish. The support level held twice, confirming that buyers are defending this price. Best when accompanied by volume.
Tweezer Top
BEARISH REVERSAL
Two candles share the same high. The first is bullish; the second is bearish. Resistance held twice. Bulls tried to extend the rally on both sessions and failed. The level is a proven ceiling.
14 / TWO-CANDLE

Kicker Patterns

Considered one of the most powerful two-candle patterns. A complete overnight reversal driven by sudden, dramatic news or sentiment shift. The gap between candles leaves no room for debate.

Bullish Kicker
VERY STRONG REVERSAL · Rarely Fails
Bearish candle followed by a bullish candle that gaps up at the open and moves strongly higher. The gap forms because sentiment shifted dramatically overnight — earnings surprise, news event, or policy change. Almost no overlap between candles.
Bearish Kicker
VERY STRONG REVERSAL · Rarely Fails
Bullish candle followed by a bearish candle that gaps down at the open and sells off strongly. All prior gains are suddenly erased as sentiment collapses. This pattern reflects complete capitulation of the bullish thesis.

17 / THREE-CANDLE

Three Inside Up & Three Inside Down

A confirmed harami. The third candle provides the evidence needed to validate what the harami merely suggested — the reversal is underway.

Three Inside Up
CONFIRMED BULLISH REVERSAL · ~65%
Bearish candle → small bullish inside → confirming bullish close above Day 1's open. The third candle is the key — it proves the reversal has follow-through.

18 / THREE-CANDLE

Abandoned Baby

The rarest and most powerful three-candle reversal pattern. A doji that is completely isolated from surrounding candles by gaps — a market that was utterly abandoned, with no overlap of any kind.

Bullish Abandoned Baby
RARE · EXTREMELY POWERFUL
Bear candle → gap down doji → gap up bullish candle. The doji is completely isolated. Both gaps (down and up) confirm complete selling exhaustion and a fresh surge of buying interest. The market abandoned the bears.
Bearish Abandoned Baby
RARE · EXTREMELY POWERFUL
Bull candle → gap up doji → gap down bearish candle. Buyers reached exhaustion, and the doji at the top is completely isolated from both sides. Bears stepped in hard, leaving bulls abandoned at the top.
19 / THREE+ CANDLE

Rising & Falling Three Methods

Continuation patterns. A strong directional candle, followed by 2–3 small counter-trend candles that stay within the range of the first, then a final candle that continues the original direction.

Rising Three Methods
BULLISH CONTINUATION
Large bull → 2–4 small counter-trend bears → large bull that closes above Day 1's high. The small pullback candles represent consolidation, not reversal. Bulls are merely resting before continuing.
Falling Three Methods
BEARISH CONTINUATION
Large bear → 2–4 small counter-trend bulls → large bear that closes below Day 1's low. The brief pullback is a bear flag in candlestick form. Sellers are taking a brief pause, not losing control.
20 / ADVANCED

Market Psychology

Every candlestick pattern is a behavioral fingerprint — a record of collective human decision-making under uncertainty. Understanding the psychology makes the patterns unforgettable.

The Fear-Greed Cycle

Candlestick patterns are best understood as visual representations of the ongoing battle between fear (bearish pressure) and greed (bullish pressure). The real body shows who won that particular battle; the shadows show how hard the loser fought.

Long Bodies
One side maintained control through the entire session. No meaningful resistance encountered. Momentum is strong. These candles belong in the trend direction.
Long Shadows
The losing side fought hard. An extreme was reached and then forcefully rejected. Long shadows at key levels are among the most reliable signals because they show active defense.
Small Bodies
Neither side could sustain an advantage. Often precedes a large directional move as the market resolves its indecision. Watch these closely at critical levels.
Gaps
Gaps between candles reflect overnight sentiment shifts — news, earnings, macro events. Gaps that fail to fill (confirmed gaps) are some of the most important technical events on a chart.

Why Reversals Require Prior Trends

A hammer at the bottom of a 3-day decline is meaningless. The same hammer after a 6-month downtrend at a major support level is highly significant. This is not arbitrary — it reflects the accumulating pressure of trapped traders. The longer and sharper the trend, the more short positions have been built (in a downtrend), and the more explosive the potential reversal when those shorts need to cover.

Nison's Core Teaching

Steve Nison emphasized that a "reversal pattern" is more accurately a "trend change pattern." Reversals rarely happen in one day. The pattern signals that psychology is shifting — but the old trend may consolidate, drift sideways, or reverse gradually over multiple candles before the new trend is fully established.

Market Context Framework

No candlestick pattern operates in a vacuum. The CMT program stresses that pattern signals must be evaluated against the backdrop of trend, support/resistance, and volume.

The Five-Context Checklist

Before acting on any candlestick signal, CMT practitioners evaluate these five contextual factors:

Factor Bullish Enhancer Signal Weakener
1. Prior Trend Extended downtrend (reversal patterns) Sideways market; very short trend
2. Location At major support, Fibonacci level, prior high/low Mid-range with no nearby structure
3. Volume Higher volume on signal day vs. prior average Lower-than-average volume = weak conviction
4. Timeframe Weekly or daily timeframe 1–5 minute charts (high noise)
5. Confirmation Next candle confirms direction Ambiguous follow-through candle

Support & Resistance Integration

The most powerful candlestick signals occur precisely at significant support and resistance levels. A shooting star at a random price level is weak. A shooting star at a 52-week high that coincides with a round number and a prior pivot is a high-probability short setup.

Volume as Confirmation

Volume validates the intention behind a pattern. High volume on a bullish engulfing candle means institutions participated — the move is meaningful. The same pattern on half-average volume might be noise. As a rule: price is the signal, volume is the conviction.

Candlesticks with Technical Indicators

The CMT approach is to use candlestick patterns as entry triggers within a broader technical framework — not as standalone systems.

With Moving Averages

A hammer at the 200-day EMA is vastly more significant than a hammer in mid-air. Use the MA as the support/resistance anchor; the candlestick provides the entry timing. Bullish patterns near a rising EMA = continuation; near a declining EMA = potential reversal with higher risk.

With RSI

A bearish engulfing pattern on RSI >70 (overbought) is a high-conviction short setup. A hammer on RSI <30 (oversold) reinforces the bullish reversal case. RSI divergence + reversal candle = strongest signal combination.

With VWAP (Intraday)

For 0DTE and intraday traders: a bullish engulfing that forms at VWAP support on the first retest of the day is among the highest-probability setups available. VWAP is the institutional reference price — reaction candles there carry extra weight.

With Bollinger Bands

A shooting star at the upper Bollinger Band confirms overextension and seller rejection simultaneously. A hammer at the lower band after the price has walked down the band is a mean-reversion setup with two layers of confirmation.

With Fibonacci

The 61.8% retracement is the most powerful Fibonacci level. A bullish reversal pattern (morning star, bullish engulfing) at this level, especially after a sharp decline, has historically high hit rates. The pattern provides the trigger; Fib provides the location confidence.

With MACD

A bearish candlestick pattern that coincides with MACD crossing below the signal line creates a multi-system confluence signal. When price action (candlestick) and momentum (MACD) agree on direction, the probability of follow-through significantly increases.

Multi-Timeframe Analysis

The most powerful candlestick setups occur when signals align across multiple timeframes — a technique called Top-Down Analysis or Multiple Time Frame (MTF) confirmation.

The Three-Timeframe Rule

CMT practitioners typically analyze three timeframes: a higher timeframe for trend identification, an intermediate timeframe for the pattern signal, and a lower timeframe for entry precision.

Trader Type Higher TF (Trend) Signal TF (Pattern) Entry TF (Timing)
Position Trader Monthly Weekly Daily
Swing Trader Weekly Daily 4-Hour
Day Trader Daily 1-Hour 15-Minute
0DTE / Scalper Daily / 30m 5-Minute 1-Minute
Practical Application

For 0DTE SPY options trading: confirm the daily trend direction first (above/below key EMAs), then look for the 5-minute setup (EMA crossover retest with a reversal candlestick pattern at VWAP or a key intraday level). The candlestick becomes the trigger within the established context — not the reason to trade.

Risk Management

Technical signals without defined risk are just guesses. The CMT framework insists that every pattern-based trade must define entry, stop, and target before execution.

1×

Never take a candlestick pattern trade with a risk-reward below 1:1. Target minimum 2:1 for single-pattern entries.

Common Errors & Pitfalls

CMT practitioners are defined as much by what they avoid as by what they do. These are the most frequent errors in applied candlestick analysis.

Error 1 — Pattern Without Trend Context

Acting on a "bullish" pattern without confirming you are in a downtrend (for reversals) or uptrend (for continuations). A hammer in a sideways market is statistically no better than a coin flip.

Error 2 — Cherry-Picking

Seeing a pattern on any chart after the fact is easy — every chart has patterns. The discipline is defining the pattern criteria in advance and only looking for that specific setup in the correct context.

Error 3 — No Confirmation Requirement

Acting on single-candle signals (especially doji and spinning tops) without waiting for a confirming candle dramatically reduces reliability from ~65% to near coin-flip levels.

Error 4 — Ignoring Volume

A bullish engulfing on 40% of average volume is a weak signal. The pattern tells you who won the battle; volume tells you how many soldiers were involved. A small battle win is meaningless in a large war.

Error 5 — Over-Pattern-ing

Forcing patterns onto charts. Not every two-candle sequence is a harami. Not every small-bodied candle is a doji. Apply strict criteria. If you have to talk yourself into it, the pattern isn't there.

Error 6 — Higher Timeframe Neglect

A bullish pattern on the 5-minute chart against a daily downtrend is trading against the primary force. Always anchor your analysis to the higher timeframe trend. Trade with the tide, not against it.
